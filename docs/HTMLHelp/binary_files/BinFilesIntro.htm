Introduction

 

This section will explain in detail what binary files are, ie, the type of data they can contain, and how it is stored.

 

The new topics on Boolean Operations, Numeric Operations, and Bit Manipulation go further by explaining ways to change binary data.  This information is useful when using the built-in HexEdit calculator (perhaps in a keystroke macro), or for evaluating expressions in a template, etc.

 

Bits and Bytes

 

All information in a computer is stored as bits, which can have a value of on or off (true or false, 1 or 0). Nowadays, almost all computers and peripherals (for many reasons) are based on storing bits in bytes or chunks of 8 bits. Admittedly, many processors (CPUs) transfer data to and from memory in chunks of 16, 32 and even 64 bits, but they invariably have some instructions that operate on a single byte even if they have to take an extra 8, 24 or 56 bits along for the ride.

 

Further, storage and transmission of data (except at the lowest levels of software and hardware) always deals with bytes. Files on disk or tape are outwardly stored as a number of bytes in a specific order (although at the physical level they may be stored as very large chunks of bits or bytes).

 

It is often necessary to identify an individual bit in a byte (or in a larger entity such as a word).  The convention is to number bits right to left starting at zero for the least significant or "low order" bit. That is, bit X stores the binary digit for 2^X. Since there are 8 bits in a byte these are numbered from 0 to 7. Similarly, the bits of a word are numbered 0 to 15, etc.

Conventions and Terms

 

Although some have been known to refer to anything from a chunk of 6 bits to a word of 16 bits as a byte, I will only refer to the more conventional 8-bit byte.  The definition of a word is even less precise, as it typically refers to the "natural" size of an integer for a specific processor so can be anything from 8 bits to 64 bits.  Typically, in an MSDOS or Windows environment a word refers to a 16-bit value.

 

To be precise here are the terms that I will use:

 

Bit = storage for one binary digit

Byte = 8 bits

Nybble = 4 bits, or half a byte

Word = 16 bits, or 2 bytes

Double-word = 32 bits, 4 bytes, or 2 words

Quad-word = 64 bits, 8 bytes, or 4 words

 

I will also use an asterisk (*) to indicate multiplication, and a caret (^) to indicate raising to a power. So 2^4 means 2 raised to the power 4 (or 2*2*2*2).

 

I will also refer to the CPU (central processing unit) as the processor.  This is the hardware at the heart of the computer that reads from memory (RAM), manipulates data and writes back to memory.  An example is the Intel Pentium series of processors.
